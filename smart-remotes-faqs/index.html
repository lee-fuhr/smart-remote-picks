Smart Remotes questions, answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A smart remote or voice remote is a universal controller that combines IR, Bluetooth, and Wi-Fi to command multiple devices from a single unit. Most modern options include integrated voice control through Alexa, Google Assistant, or Siri, making it possible to search content, adjust volume, or launch apps hands-free. The right choice depends on your existing equipment, the voice assistant you already use, and whether you prefer a dedicated remote or a phone-based app.

What is a smart remote and how does it work?

A smart remote replaces your TV, soundbar, streaming stick, and gaming console remotes with one device that talks to multiple pieces of gear. Instead of only blasting infrared signals like an old universal remote, smart remotes also use Bluetooth and Wi-Fi. This lets them control devices that don’t have traditional IR sensors, such as a Roku, Apple TV, or Fire TV streaming stick, and even turn on lights or adjust a thermostat via smart-home hubs. Most smart remotes pair with an app for initial setup. You tell the app what devices you own, and it either fetches the correct codes from an online database or walks you through a learning process where you point the original remote at your smart remote to copy the commands. After that, all your equipment can be activated by a single “Watch TV” activity or by pressing a source button. Voice control adds another layer: press the mic button and say “Launch Netflix” or “Turn up the volume” and the remote sends the right commands to the appropriate device.

Do I need a smart remote if my TV already has one?

It depends on how many devices you use regularly. If you only have a TV and nothing else, no soundbar, no streaming box, no console, your TV’s standard remote is probably sufficient. But once you add a soundbar, a Roku, a Blu-ray player, or a game console, you end up juggling three or four clickers. A smart remote consolidates all those into one, even handling activities like “Watch Blu-ray” that automatically turn on the TV, switch inputs, and set the correct audio mode. Another reason to consider a smart remote is voice control. Many built-in TV remotes now have wake-word or button-activated voice search, but they are usually limited to that TV’s platform. A third-party smart remote can control voice actions across devices from different brands, letting you say “Play The Crown on Netflix” regardless of whether the TV is a Vizio, Sony, or TCL. If clutter or cross-platform voice commands matter to you, a smart remote may be worth the investment.

What are the differences between IR, Bluetooth, and Wi-Fi remotes?

The type of connection a smart remote uses determines which devices it can control and how reliably it works. Infrared (IR) is the oldest and simplest method: the remote shoots out invisible light that the front of the device must see. IR doesn’t require pairing and works on practically any TV or older soundbar, but it needs a clear line of sight and won’t penetrate cabinets or walls. Bluetooth remotes, such as those shipped with Apple TV, Fire TV, and many Roku models, pair directly with the streaming device. They don’t need line of sight, so you can tuck the box behind a TV. However, Bluetooth won’t control your TV’s power or volume unless the streaming device supports HDMI-CEC to relay those commands. Wi-Fi remotes talk over your home network and can command devices that aren’t in the same room, plus they often integrate with smart-home systems. Many premium universal remotes (like the now-discontinued Logitech Harmony line) combine IR, Bluetooth, and Wi-Fi in one hub, letting the remote talk to the hub wirelessly while the hub blasts IR and Bluetooth to your gear.

Which voice assistant works best with smart remotes?

The two major ecosystems are Amazon Alexa and Google Assistant, with Apple Siri appearing mainly in Apple TV remotes and HomeKit-compatible hubs. Alexa is found on many third-party universal remotes and on Amazon’s Fire TV remotes; it’s particularly good for searching Prime Video, controlling Alexa-compatible lights, and setting timers. Google Assistant is built into Android TV and Google TV remotes, as well as some universal options; it excels at YouTube, Google Search, and controlling Nest or other Google Home devices. Your best bet is to stick with the assistant you already use in your house. If you have Echo speakers everywhere, a remote with Alexa will feel natural. If you’re deep in Google’s world, a Google Assistant remote keeps consistent phrasing and routines. Most modern streaming remotes also support both assistants via dedicated buttons or by long-pressing the mic button, but you may lose some deep-link functionality if you use the “wrong” assistant. For a universal remote that works across multiple platforms, look for one that explicitly lists support for your preferred assistant, and be aware that some budget models reduce the mic to basic search only.

How to set up and program a universal smart remote?

Setup usually starts with downloading the remote manufacturer’s companion app on your phone. The app will ask what brand and model each device is, then it either pulls codes from the cloud or sends a series of test signals. Most newer remotes automate this with a library of thousands of devices, so you simply tap a brand name and the remote tries a few power commands until the device turns off, you confirm it worked and move on. Some remotes also offer a learning mode: you point the original remote at the universal remote’s learning eye and press each button you want copied (power, volume, input, etc.). This is useful for obscure or vintage gear not in the database. After programming, you can set up “activities”. one-touch sequences that power on the TV, switch the soundbar to the correct input, and tune the streaming box to the home screen. If you get stuck, most manufacturers have video walkthroughs on their websites. Expect to spend 15 to 30 minutes for a simple setup; a full home theater with six devices may take an hour.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a smart remote with my older TV that doesn’t have HDMI-CEC?

Yes. Older TVs rely solely on IR, and most smart remotes include an IR blaster either built into the remote or in a hub. You just need to point the remote or place the hub where it has a clear line of sight to the TV’s IR sensor. The remote won’t be able to read a menu or control on-screen items via CEC, but all basic functions like power, volume, and input switching will work.

Will a voice remote work if my TV is not a smart TV?

It depends on the remote. If the voice remote is designed for a specific streaming device (such as a Fire TV stick or Roku), it will work with that box, but the TV itself doesn’t need to be smart, the streaming device handles the voice commands. For a universal remote that includes voice, the mic signal is usually processed by the remote’s companion hub or a connected smartphone, not the TV itself, so a dumb TV can still receive voice-initiated commands via IR or Bluetooth.

Can I control my soundbar and TV with one smart remote?

Absolutely, that is one of the primary reasons people buy a smart remote. You set up an activity that tells the remote to turn on the TV, turn on the soundbar, and set the TV’s audio output to optical or HDMI-ARC. After that, a single button press does everything. Many smart remotes also let you program separate volume buttons that control the soundbar regardless of which device is the active source.

Do smart remotes work with streaming devices like Apple TV or Chromecast?

Yes, but compatibility varies. Apple TV uses Bluetooth and also responds to IR for basic commands, so many smart remotes can control it via a hub that emits both signals. Chromecast (especially the newer Google TV models) uses Bluetooth and Wi-Fi; some universal remotes have direct support, while others rely on HDMI-CEC to talk to the TV. Before buying, check the remote’s device compatibility list, most major universal remotes support Apple TV and Chromecast with Google TV.

What is the best budget smart remote?

On a budget, many users find good results with the SofaBaton U1, which offers a small base unit and a simple remote that handles IR and Bluetooth for up to a dozen devices. For even less, the RCA Universal Remote series covers basic IR-only needs. Keep in mind that budget models usually lack voice control or full activity automation, and they may require manual code entry rather than a smartphone app setup.

Can a smart remote turn on my TV if the TV is in standby mode?

Yes. If the remote uses IR, the power command turns on any TV that is plugged in and in standby, no special feature needed. For Bluetooth or Wi-Fi remotes, they can send a wake command through HDMI-CEC if the TV supports it, or they can still blast an IR power signal (most hybrid remotes include an IR blaster for this purpose). Just make sure the remote has line of sight or the hub is positioned near the TV’s sensor.

In shortThe best smart remote is the one that fits your current devices and your preferred voice assistant, not the one with the longest feature list. Start by writing down all the gear you own and how you want them to work together, one-touch activity, voice search, or simple input switching, then look for a remote that supports those exact needs. Whether you choose a dedicated universal remote or a voice-enabled streaming remote, the goal is fewer clickers and more seamless control.
